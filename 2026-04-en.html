Dear colleagues,

After a long pause since the July newsletter last year, we are picking things up again. With Raimon’s departure and my start on 1 October, a new phase begins for DFG Sustainability, in which we are building a meaningful translation of our IMPACT2030 strategy into practice.

Over the past months, I have had the opportunity to get to know DFG and the sector better, characterised by strong craftsmanship, engaged colleagues and entrepreneurship. At the same time, I see that this goes hand in hand with a number of significant challenges. The recent findings by the NVWA regarding the use of crop protection products in the chain, the rising tensions in the Middle East and the limitations on transit via the Strait of Hormuz illustrate how vulnerable the supply chain is. This requires us to stay committed to our sustainability ambitions, while also remaining agile and realistic about what is achievable now and what should be prioritised.

In the coming period, we will bring more focus and alignment internally. Through dedicated taskforces, we are accelerating four key themes within IMPACT2030 (Carbon, Circular Packaging, Social Impact & Crop Protection), while ensuring that DFG companies can translate these into their day-to-day operations. Communication and actively sharing experiences with one another will be an essential part of this.

With this first edition of our renewed quarterly newsletter, we are reintroducing a regular rhythm in how we inform and engage with each other on the steps we are taking together, in a world that is constantly evolving.
Linda van Waveren
Group Sustainability Manager
Dutch Flower Group

DFG continues to move towards 100% FSI-certified sourcing
By Linda van Waveren and Jonny Young

Thanks to the joint efforts of procurement, sustainability teams and management, we made another significant step in 2025 towards our IMPACT2030 targets. As DFG, we closed the year with 81% FSI-certified flowers and plants, a strong group result.

At the end of March, Royal FloraHolland reaffirmed its strategy, including additional support for growers and financial incentives for non-certified supply. This approach accelerates movement across the chain and aligns directly with our own strategy.

Supply chain dialogue on social topics in the sector
By Linda van Waveren

On 4 March, FSI, Rabobank, VGB and Dutch Flower Group organised the Social Dialogue International Floriculture in Hoofddorp. Over forty participants from the international floriculture supply chain came together to discuss strengthening social responsibility in the chain.

The session opened with a contribution from Leontine Treur on labour market dynamics and a call from Vivian Vaessen (FNV) for shared responsibility. Participants described the dialogue as inspiring and constructive.

The outcomes of the dialogue will serve as input for next steps, including further preparations towards a new SER sector agreement on international responsible business conduct.

Working smarter with energy
By Marjon van Diepen

Since 2022, all Dutch DFG companies have been using green electricity, and since 2024 JZ Flowers in the UK has joined as well. This is an important step in reducing our footprint. However, more can be done.

To gain better insight into energy consumption per company, Michiel van Eerden and Harry Brockhoff have worked over the past month on a fair allocation model. In three groups – large consumers, small consumers and plant companies – stakeholders were presented with insights into purchased energy and total kWh usage across DFG.

SKAL certification: a step forward in sustainable and responsible business
By Marco van Kesteren

The Floral Connection has recently obtained SKAL certification for organic products: preparation, distribution/placing on the market, and storage of organic flowers and plants. This certification confirms that our processes comply with strict European regulations for organic production and processing. It ensures transparency and reliability throughout our supply chain, from procurement to delivery, and supports the expansion of our sustainable product offering for retail.

AIPH nominations
By Iris de Waal

At the end of January, part of the Herburg Roses and Nini team took the stage in Germany during the AIPH awards ceremony. Although we did not take home the awards this time, we are extremely proud of our two nominations in the categories Sustainability and Cut Flowers and Bulbs.

The team was recognised for its efforts in innovation in sea freight, the ambition towards chemical-free cultivation, and our social projects: the Herburg Roses Special Needs School and the Nini daycare.

Coloríginz organises Organic Day
By Olga Keijser

Last month, we organised the Coloríginz Organic Day: an inspiring event where organic growers and market parties came together to share knowledge, insights and ambitions. What stood out most was the genuine interest and engagement of all participants. The energy in the room showed that organic is no longer a niche topic, but a shared movement that everyone actively wants to contribute to.

Wild Harvest on the path to certification
By Olga Keijser and Jonny Young

In January, Jonny Young (Human Rights Lead at DFG), together with Olga Keijser (Sustainability Manager at Coloríginz), travelled to Spain to visit one of our key exporters in the Wild Harvest pistachio supply chain.

The visit provided a valuable opportunity to engage directly with workers across the chain, from pickers and landowners to exporters, to gain better insight into the realities of wild harvesting and to assess the progress and impact of the FSI Wild Harvest Project.
